Building a coherent argument

Writing coherently means creating an easily readable piece of writing that flows smoothly, that is logically organised and that has ideas that are connected effectively.
This means creating paragraphs that develop your main argument and link together to build coherence.

Let’s explore how you can make your academic writing more coherent.

Structure your work

There are many types of academic writing, but one thing they usually have in common is that they have an introduction, a body and a conclusion:

    • Your introduction should contain a section which is called a thesis statement. This is where you tell the reader what the main argument is and the topics you will cover.
    • In the body paragraphs, you develop and support your main argument.
    • The conclusion reminds your reader what your thesis or main argument is.

In the Assessments section, you can find out more about structuring assignments such as essays, reports and literature reviews.

TEEL paragraph structure

This structure is commonly used for body paragraphs in academic writing. The TEEL acronym stands for:

  • Topic sentence
  • Explanation
  • Evidence and/or examples
  • Link (the paragraph to the next paragraph)

The Writing paragraphs page has a detailed explanation of TEEL paragraphs.

Key word and phrases

Keywords and concepts should connect the topic and linking sentences in each paragraph to the next. This can be done through the repetition of key words or terms, and by using synonyms (words and phrases that have a similar meaning).

Linking word and phrases

Using linking words and phrases explains to your reader the connection between your ideas. For example, some linking words and phrases show contrast (however, in spite of), while others show addition (additionally, moreover) and others introduce examples (to illustrate, for example).

For more information, visit the Linking words page.

Demonstrative adjectives

This, that, these and those are used to refer to something that has previously been mentioned.
For example:

  • Pemberton (2023) describes a system that could be implemented into the education of high school students. This system has many benefits.
  • To strengthen their argument, Lim (2024) cites research conducted by Abdul. However, this study was done over 20 years ago and is not current.

Relative pronouns

Words such as which, that, who and where are relative pronouns. They are used to link parts of a sentence together, creating cohesion in the sentence. In the following example, the first part of the sentence mentions micromanagement. Later, which also refers to micromanagement:

Micromanagement is a management style which can lead to inefficiencies and high turnover rates in an organisation.
