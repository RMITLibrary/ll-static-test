Building a coherent argument

Writing coherently means creating an easily readable piece of writing that flows smoothly, is logically organised and has ideas that are connected effectively. This means creating paragraphs that develop your main argument and link together to build coherence. Let’s explore how you can make your academic writing more coherent.

Structure your work

There are many types of academic writing, but one thing they usually have in common is that they have an introduction, a body and a conclusion:

  • Your introduction should contain a section which is called a thesis statement. This is where you tell the reader what the main argument is and the topics you will cover.
  • In the body paragraphs, you develop and support your main argument.
  • The conclusion reminds your reader what your thesis or main argument is.

In the Assessments section, you can find out more about structuring assignments such as essays, reports and literature reviews.

This global connectivity fosters a diverse exchange of perspectives and skills, leading to innovative outcomes that might not be possible in isolation. Paragraphs

In academic writing, each paragraph should focus on a distinct topic, and you should start a new paragraph when introducing a new topic.

Organising body paragraphs logically is important; group paragraphs exploring the same theme together before moving on to different themes. The first sentence of your paragraphs (the topic sentence), should clearly state what will be discussed in the paragraph.

Creating an essay plan can help ensure each paragraph has a clear focus and follows a logical sequence. To do this:

  • list the topics of each paragraph
  • check for logical grouping of topics
  • check that all paragraph topics are relevant to your assignment question and support your argument effectively.

Here's an example of an essay plan that a student has made to ensure their work flows logically.

    Thesis statement

  • Urbanisation offers employment opportunities but also poses challenges like environmental degradation, social inequality, and infrastructural strain, requiring sustainable planning.

Body paragraph 1 topic

  • Urbanisation drives economic growth through job creation and innovation.

Body paragraph 2 topic

  • It often leads to environmental degradation, necessitating green policies.

Body paragraph 3 topic

  • Urbanisation can increase social inequality by limiting access to essential services for marginalised communities.

Body paragraph 4 topic

  • Infrastructural strain from urbanisation underscores the need for comprehensive urban planning.

Conclusion

  • Urbanisation's benefits and challenges must be balanced through sustainable and inclusive measures.

TEEL paragraph structure

We have already seen that it is important to have a clear topic sentence for your paragraph. Now, let's explore how the rest of your paragraph can support your topic sentence.

One way we can do this is by using the TEEL structure, which is commonly used for body paragraphs in academic writing. It stands for:

  • Topic sentence
  • Explanation
  • Evidence and/or examples
  • Link (the paragraph to the next paragraph)

The Writing paragraphs page has a detailed explanation of TEEL paragraphs.
